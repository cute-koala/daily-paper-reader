# Learning from World Feedback: Why Model Uncertainty Fails as a Risk Signal in Model-Based RL 

Zhaohui Wang 1

# Abstract 

The RLxF programme argues that learning sig-nals should come from world feedback rather than from internal model proxies. We instantiate this position in safe model-based control and distil it into three concrete design principles. Empirically, across four world-model architectures spanning a 2× MSE range, MPC planning is statistically equivalent (TOST, n=200 ), and dynamics-based uncertainty penalties increase collision rates from 

26% to 34% : the standard MBRL safety proxy is anti-correlated with safety in this regime. Replac-ing the model-internal proxy with three world-feedback signals (a sensor-derived margin via minimum lidar, a temporal signal via time-to-collision, and an outcome-supervised feedback model gψ trained on prior collision labels, struc-turally analogous to outcome-trained reward mod-els in RLHF) reduces collisions to 1–14% without retraining the world model or the planner. The mechanism is structural: model uncertainty has support over state-prediction space, whereas task risk has support over constraint boundaries, with empirical correlation r < 0.15 . From this we ex-tract three RLxF principles (ground risk in world outcomes, validate proxies before deployment, and substitute outcome-trained feedback models when direct world signals are unavailable) and argue they apply equally to model-based control and to verifier-based or RLHF approaches in LLM alignment.

We ask the corresponding ques-tion for model-based control: does the same principle hold when the world feedback is mechanical rather than human, and the model is a learned dynamics model rather than a base LLM? 

The default practice in MBRL is to (i) select the world model to minimise prediction error, and (ii) when safety is required, repurpose the model’s own uncertainty as a risk signal (Yu et al., 2020; Kidambi et al., 2020; Janner et al., 2019; Malik et al., 2019). Both choices treat the model as a proxy for the world. Wei et al. The contrast is structural rather than empirical accident: dy-namics uncertainty measures variance in state-prediction space , while collision risk concentrates at constraint bound-aries , and the two are nearly orthogonal under partial ob-servability ( r < 0.15 ). 

Three RLxF principles. We extract three design principles, formalised in Section 5 and supported by the experiments in Sections 3 and 4: 

P1. Ground risk in world outcomes. An internal proxy (model uncertainty, token entropy) is unsafe as a risk signal unless its alignment with the actual outcome label has been validated. Default to world-observed outcomes (collision events, verifier results) first. 

P2. Validate proxies via outcome correlation. Before de-ploying a candidate signal f in the planner cost (or as a reward model), measure corr (f, r outcome ) on a held-out batch. Low correlation predicts deployment failure; high correlation predicts deployment success. 

P3. Use outcome-supervised feedback models when direct signals are unavailable. A small model gψ trained on outcome labels recovers most of the benefit of di-rect world feedback; this is the model-based-control analogue of outcome-trained reward models in RLHF (Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022). 

Contributions. (1) A controlled cross-architecture demon-stration that, in a partially observable navigation POMDP, prediction MSE and MPC planning quality are decou-pled (TOST equivalence, n=200 ), violating the implicit MBRL assumption underlying P1 . (2) Evidence that dynamics-uncertainty penalties increase collisions in the same regime, demonstrating the cost of skipping P2 .(3) A world-feedback procedure (minimum lidar, time-to-collision, outcome-supervised feedback model gψ ) that re-duces collisions to 1–14% without retraining the model or planner, instantiating P3 . (4) A structural diagnostic, the per-state correlation between a candidate signal and the out-come label, that operationalises P2 as a pre-deployment check and extends naturally to LLM verifier and reward-model selection.

# 4. World Feedback Beats Model Confidence 

If model accuracy is not the determining factor, what is? We hold the model and planner fixed and vary only the risk term R(τ ) in the MPC cost, comparing one model-internal proxy ( dynamics uncertainty ) against three world-feedback signals. 

Signals. 

• Dynamics uncertainty σdyn (τ ): ensemble disagreement of the world-model rollout along τ . This is the standard MOPO-style (Yu et al., 2020) risk proxy. • Minimum lidar along τ : min t min b ℓt,b where ℓt,b is the predicted distance return on beam b at step t. World-grounded geometric feedback. • Time-to-collision (TTC): predicted time until first li-dar return falls below a threshold along τ . Decision-relevant temporal feedback. • Outcome-supervised feedback model gψ : a 2-layer MLP on (s, a ) trained on collision labels from past episodes, predicting per-step collision probability. Structurally identical to an outcome-supervised reward model in RLHF (Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022): a small classifier trained on world-observed outcome labels and queried by the planner / decoder at inference. We refer to it as a feedback model through-out to make the RLxF analogy explicit. Table 2 shows the comparison. Dynamics-uncertainty penal-ties increase collisions monotonically with λ (26% →

34% ), the opposite of the intended effect. All three world-feedback signals reduce collisions, with minimum lidar reaching 1% at λ=5 . The signals’ AUC against ground-truth collision labels predicts their ranking ( 1.00 > 0.97 > 0.60 ).

Why dynamics uncertainty fails here. Dynamics uncer-tainty is high where the model is unsure about state evo-lution: where lidar geometry is ambiguous, where partial observability hides obstacle dynamics, or where training data is sparse. Collision risk is high where the robot’s state trajectory crosses a constraint boundary. These two events have markedly different supports: the per-state Pearson correlation between σdyn and ground-truth collision prox-imity is r < 0.15 in our setting. Penalising σdyn therefore steers the planner toward predictable but not necessarily safe regions; in narrow corridors with dynamic obstacles, the displacement concentrates trajectories near hazards. 

What world-feedback signals provide that the model can-not. All three world-feedback signals are computed against quantities the world reveals to the agent: lidar returns, the constraint label produced when an episode terminates, and the time-derivative of the same lidar measurements. None requires the model to predict where the robot will be; they require only that the planner evaluate where the candidate trajectory is relative to measured world structure. In the language of Wei et al. (2024)’s taxonomy, this is neither dis-tribution correction, control-as-inference, value-equivalence, nor differentiable planning; it is a fifth category that replaces the model-internal risk channel with a directly-measured world feedback channel. 

# 5. RLxF Principles and Implications 

The RLxF programme asks when an agent should ground its learning in world feedback rather than in internal prox-ies. The experiments above instantiate this question in safe model-based control and support three concrete principles. We argue that the principles transfer directly to LLM align-ment, and we present them as a pre-deployment diagnostic toolkit.

Section 4 shows that σdyn used as a safety penalty makes collisions worse , because the proxy’s support (open regions, low data density) does not coincide with the constraint-boundary support of the actual outcome. 

Principle 2: Validate proxies before deployment. A can-didate signal f should be admitted as a risk penalty only if it passes a held-out alignment test against the outcome label. The natural diagnostic is the per-state correlation corr (f, r outcome ) or, for binary outcomes, the AUC of f

against the label. Table 2 confirms this: the AUC order-ing ( 1.00 > 0.97 > 0.60 ) predicts the collision-mitigation ordering. A signal with AUC near 0.5 is no better than chance and may, as σdyn does here, be anti-aligned. 

Principle 3: Use outcome-supervised feedback models when direct world signals are unavailable. When the world’s outcome signal is sparse or available only post-hoc, train a small feedback model gψ on past outcome labels and use gψ as a substitute. Justification: our 2-layer MLP collision predictor, trained on ∼50K episode-termination labels, reduces the collision rate by an order of magnitude (26% → 14% at λ=5 ) without retraining the world model or planner. This is the model-based-control analogue of the outcome-trained reward models that underpinned the transition from RL-from-likelihood to RL-from-feedback in language modelling (Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022; Christiano et al., 2017). 

5.2. Generalization to LLM Alignment 

The principles are not specific to navigation. The mapping to LLM alignment is direct: Under this mapping, our principles instantiate as follows. 

P1 : sequence likelihood and token entropy are not safety signals by default. P2 : a candidate verifier or content clas-sifier should be AUC-validated against the actual harm or failure label before being used as a reward. P3 : when the verifier cannot be queried online or the outcome is delayed, an outcome-supervised reward model is the principled sub-stitute. P1 and P3 are established design patterns in LLM alignment (Christiano et al., 2017; Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022); P2 is more often implicit. What our control experiments add is empirical evidence that the three principles are not merely guidance but structurally neces-sary in regimes with hidden latent variables (Section I): the model-internal proxy and the outcome have different sup-ports, so any deployment without P2 validation relies on coincidental alignment. 

5.3. An RLxF Diagnostic Toolkit 

Combining P1–P3 yields a deployment checklist for a can-didate signal f proposed as a planner penalty or as a reward model: 1. Compute corr (f, r outcome ) (or AUC for binary out-comes) on a held-out labelled batch. 2. Reject if |corr | < τ (we use τ =0 .3, AUC < 0.65 ). A 

σdyn -style proxy in our POMDP would fail this gate (corr = 0 .108 , AUC = 0 .60 ). 3. If f passes, deploy at the smallest λ for which the safety target is met (avoid over-penalising, which sup-presses goal-reaching without further safety gain). 4. At runtime, monitor the empirical AUC of f against post-hoc outcome labels; if it degrades, fall back to a more directly-measured signal (sensor margin, verifier check). The toolkit operationalises the RLxF design pattern: world feedback is the primary source of supervision, internal prox-ies are subject to outcome-correlation validation, and feed-back models are a principled substitute when world feedback is unavailable online. 

5.4. Scope and Limitations Scope. The phenomenon, model-side gains failing to propa-gate to the policy while world-feedback signals dominate, is bounded to partially observable settings with non-smooth costs. In fully observable, smooth-dynamics benchmarks, MSE and planning are correlated and dynamics-uncertainty penalties behave more sensibly. Our claim is not that world feedback always wins, but that it dominates in the regimes RLxF targets: real systems with hidden latent variables, hard constraints, and economically meaningful failure events. 

Limitations. Observations are lidar-based; pixel-based perception is untested. The lidar and TTC signals are environment-specific, a feature for P1 but one that requires per-domain instrumentation. The feedback model gψ is the most transferable of the three; understanding when gψ alone suffices, and when it must be combined with verifier-style direct signals, is a natural next question. # A. Statistical Procedure 

Two One-Sided Tests (TOST). TOST (Schuirmann, 1987) tests two one-sided hypotheses 

H01 : μ1 − μ2 ≤ − ∆, H02 : μ1 − μ2 ≥ ∆,

and equivalence within margin ∆ is declared when both are rejected at level α. We use ∆=1 .0 reward unit, approximately 

25% of the typical [−5, −1] reward range observed across architectures on PartialNavEnv. The choice of ∆ is conservative in that a smaller ∆ would only strengthen the equivalence finding (rejecting equivalence in a narrower band). With n=200 

pooled episodes per architecture, the TOST has > 0.95 power against an effect of 0.5 reward units at α=0 .05 (computed via bootstrap on observed variance). 

Two-sample non-parametric test. Where we test for a difference rather than for equivalence (e.g. Ensemble vs. Pure Learned in Table 1), we use the two-sided Mann–Whitney U test at α=0 .05 . The MW-U test is preferred over Welch’s t for episode rewards because the reward distribution is skewed by collision-truncated episodes. 

Paired comparisons under per-episode seeding. The risk-signal table (Table 2) uses per-cell paired episodes: for a fixed episode seed, the same initial pose, goal, and obstacle layout are evaluated under every (λ, R) cell. The relevant null is the paired-difference null, tested with the paired Wilcoxon signed-rank test on per-episode collision indicators. The reported 

p < 10 −4 for σdyn vs. collision predictor at λ=5 is from this paired test on n=200 episode pairs. 

Bootstrap confidence intervals. 95% CIs reported as x ± e in tables are from 10 ,000 percentile-bootstrap resamples at the episode level; the ±e value is the half-width of the 95% interval (mean of the two tails about the point estimate). # B. Environment Specification 

B.1. PartialNavEnv (main environment) 

The main environment is a 2D point-robot navigation POMDP. The arena is a 10 m × 10 m box. Per episode, 8 static circular obstacles (radii uniformly in [0 .3, 0.8] m) and 4 dynamic obstacles (same radii, velocity ≤ 0.6 m/s, reflecting off walls) are placed at random positions outside a 1.5 m start exclusion. The goal is a 0.5 m radius disc sampled at least 4 m from the start. 

State and observation. True state st = ( x, y, θ, v x, v y , ˙θ) ∈ R6. The agent never observes st directly. The observation 

ot ∈ R37 contains: • 32 -beam 360 ◦ lidar with max range 5 m and Gaussian noise of σ=0 .02 m, • the relative goal vector in the egocentric frame ( R2), • the egocentric velocities (ˆ v∥, ˆv⊥, ˙θ) with additive noise σ=0 .05 .

Action and dynamics. Actions at ∈ [−1, 1] 3 are mapped to (vx, v y , ˙θ) targets via a unicycle parameterisation; integration is forward-Euler at ∆t=0 .1 s for 10 inner steps per environment step. Obstacle dynamics are updated with the agent step. The agent collides if its position is within obstacle radius plus a 0.05 m safety pad. 

Reward. rt = −0.01 ∥pt − g∥2 − 0.002 ∥at∥2 + rterminal , where the terminal reward is +5 on goal contact and −5 on collision; both events terminate the episode. Maximum episode length is 200 steps; time-out yields no terminal bonus. 

Why this environment. The combination of (i) lidar-only sensing (no obstacle dynamics observed), (ii) hard collision penalty at a sharp boundary, and (iii) dynamic obstacles that act as nuisance latent variables produces the regime our paper targets: partial observability plus a non-smooth cost. Removing any one of these eliminates the misalignment we report (see Section G). 

B.2. RegimeSwitchPendulum (non-collision robustness) 

To show that the phenomenon is not collision-specific, we use a control POMDP without any collision events. RegimeSwitch-Pendulum is a standard inverted-pendulum balancing task with one modification: the damping coefficient ζ ∈ { ζlow , ζ high } is sampled once per episode and not observed . Observations are (sin θ, cos θ, ˙θ); actions are bounded torques. The agent must control across an angular threshold at ±θ∗ where the unobserved damping switches behaviour. There are no collisions and no constraint discontinuities; only a hidden environmental parameter and an angle-dependent dynamical regime. Despite an 8.15 × MSE gap between RSSM and Pure Learned, planning performance is statistically equivalent (MW-U p =0 .42 ,

# I. Why Dynamics Uncertainty Fails: A Structural Argument 

This appendix collects the informal mathematical sketch that motivates the empirical results. We give it here for completeness; a fully formal version with assumptions and bounds is the subject of a companion paper. Let rcoll (s, a ) = I[collision (s, a )] ∈ { 0, 1} be the ground-truth collision indicator, and let σ(s, a ) be the model’s prediction uncertainty about s′ ∼ p(·| s, a ). Define their joint distribution over reachable (s, a ) pairs. A safety-relevant penalty must satisfy 

P[rcoll (s, a )=1 | σ(s, a ) small ] ≤ P[rcoll (s, a )=1] .

This is a marginal calibration property: low uncertainty should not co-occur with high risk more often than chance . No general result in MBRL guarantees this property; it must hold as a contingent fact about the model and the environment. 11 Learning from World Feedback in Model-Based RL  

> Figure 3. Spatial activation maps of candidate risk signals on a representative episode. Dynamics uncertainty (top-left) activates in open space; lidar-based signals (top-right, bottom-left) activate at boundaries; the learned predictor (bottom-right) activates immediately before contact. The geometric supports of the signals are markedly different.

When does the property fail? Two structural conditions are sufficient for the failure we observe: 1. Partial observability decouples σ from boundary geometry. Under partial observability, σ(s, a ) depends on the model’s belief over latent state, which is a function of recent observations. In our environment, the observation is dominated by lidar within the agent’s current 5 m horizon; obstacles that have been observed and tracked have low σ (they are predictable), whereas the model’s residual disagreement is largest in interpolated open regions where small belief perturbations propagate (regions with low data density). Consequently, σ is low at boundaries. 2. Constraint boundaries are sharp. A small change in a at the right s flips rcoll from 0 to 1. Thus rcoll is concentrated on a measure-zero (in s) but action-rich set, while σ is distributed smoothly over (s, a ) by the model class. Under (1) and (2), σ and rcoll have different supports in (s, a ), and Pearson correlation collapses; penalising σ in the MPC cost displaces the planner along iso-σ surfaces, which are unrelated to constraint boundaries. The displacement can be in either direction; in our environment it increases collisions because the planner exchanges open-region certainty for additional narrow-corridor risk. 

Why world-feedback signals are structurally aligned. A world-feedback signal R(τ ) is computed against quantities measured by the world: lidar returns, contact events, time-derivatives of contact distances. By construction its support coincides with the support of the constraint set. Min-lidar is a noisy upper bound on ρ; TTC is a discrete-time analogue; the learned predictor is a smoothing of rcoll . None of them require the planner to predict latent state; they only require the rollout to evaluate world-measured quantities at the predicted state. The cost of the planner’s prediction errors is bounded by how badly ˆst disagrees with st, not by the size of the latent-state uncertainty. 

Implied selection rule. For a candidate signal f , the diagnostic is corr (f, r coll ) on a held-out dataset. Signals with high correlation transfer; signals with low correlation are unsafe to use as MPC penalties regardless of their accuracy at the nominal prediction task. In Table 2 we report this correlation as the AUC (signal vs. collision label); it is a reliable predictor of empirical effectiveness. 

# J. Negative Results: Ranking-Aware Training 

A natural alternative to changing the planner is to change the training objective so that the model learns to be accurate on the trajectories that matter for ranking. We tried two variants and report them as negative results. 

V1: self-prediction ranking proxy. Augment the MSE loss with Lrank (s, a 1, a 2) = max(0 , m − ( ˆC(τ1) − ˆC(τ2)) ·

sgn (C∗(τ1) − C∗(τ2))) , where ˆC is the model-rollout cost and C∗ is the proxy ground-truth cost computed from the rollout itself (not the environment). Result: training collapsed; the model learned to produce identical rollouts for every action, trivially minimising the ranking margin. The proxy is self-referential. 

V2: environment-grounded ranking. Replace the self-proxy with the true environment cost of each rollout: Lrank as above but with C∗ from environment-true execution. Result: MSE worsens from 0.037 to 0.315 (the ranking gradient pulls 12 Learning from World Feedback in Model-Based RL 

the model away from local accuracy), while the empirical ranking inversion rate of model-predicted-best vs. environment-best action is unchanged ( 94% ). The model cannot improve ranking because the source of ranking error is not a misallocation of model capacity; it is irreducible environmental uncertainty about hidden parameters. 

Diagnosis. Both failures share a cause: in this partially observable regime, the source of pairwise ranking error is common-mode bias contributed by unobservable variables (obstacle dynamics, hidden damping). No training-side intervention can predict variables that are not observed. The implication is that the fix must be on the planning side, where the planner can be given access to feedback that does not need to be predicted. This is the empirical motivation for the world-feedback approach in this paper.

# L. Extended Related Work 

Objective mismatch. Wei et al. (2024) unify a body of prior work (Lambert et al., 2020; Farahmand et al., 2017; Grimm et al., 2020) into four solution families: distribution correction (relative weighting of training transitions or weighted MLE), control-as-inference (planning and learning unified as probabilistic inference), value-equivalence (matching the model on value-relevant quantities only (Grimm et al., 2020)), and differentiable planning (gradient-through-planner training). All four families retain the safety and decision signal inside the model. Our work takes a complementary direction: we hold the model and planner fixed and replace the model-internal safety channel with a world-measured one. The result is a fifth category in the taxonomy, complementary to the existing four. 

Safety in MBRL. Dynamics-uncertainty penalties were introduced as conservative regularisers in offline MBRL (MOPO (Yu et al., 2020), MOReL (Kidambi et al., 2020)). Our finding that σdyn is anti-correlated with collision under partial observability is consistent with the conservative-RL literature’s recognition that uncertainty-as-cost can be miscalibrated 13 Learning from World Feedback in Model-Based RL 

(Chua et al., 2018; Malik et al., 2019; Kuleshov et al., 2018; Guo et al., 2017), but to our knowledge has not been demonstrated in a controlled cross-architecture/cross-signal study against task-aligned alternatives. 

Sensor-derived risk signals. Reactive controllers in robotics have long used sensor-derived margins as safety signals (e.g. artificial potential fields, dynamic-window methods). Our contribution is not the signals themselves but the structural framing: in the partially observable regime where MBRL most needs safety guarantees, the planner should be supplied with 

measured world feedback rather than modelled state evolution. 

RLxF and outcome-supervised reward modeling. The workshop framing emphasises that learning signals should be grounded in observable world feedback rather than in self-generated proxies. Our principle P3 (outcome-supervised feedback models) is the model-based-control instance of the design pattern that underpinned the transition from RL-from-likelihood to RL-from-feedback in language modelling (Christiano et al., 2017; Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022): a small classifier trained on world-observed outcome labels is queried by the agent (here, the planner; there, the decoder) in place of an internal proxy. The mechanism is the same in both domains: the support of the outcome label and the support of the internal proxy can be nearly disjoint (Section I), so without the pre-deployment validation of P2 , any internal proxy relies on coincidental alignment. The world-feedback signal here is mechanical rather than human-provided, but the structural argument carries: the signals that drive policy improvement are those that the world reveals directly, not those that a learned model can reconstruct.
